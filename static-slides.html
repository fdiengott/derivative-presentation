A short talk on financial derivatives

Moving risk

Forwards · Futures · Options · Swaps · Credit default swaps

Caveats: This presentation is me learning in public so excuse some depth of knowledge. My goal for this presentation is fully to build a strong conceptual understanding of each of these instruments, so there will be some lack of precision and simplification of the plumbing for the sake of conceptual clarity.
Five instruments, one idea. By the end they should all feel like obvious, humane inventions — not Wall Street wizardry.

Movement 1 · The ache

A farmer walks out to a perfect harvest.

And feels despair.

A great crop everywhere means low prices — they won't recoup their costs.

The counterintuitive beat: abundance is the problem. Price is set by everyone's harvest, so a bumper crop across the whole region drives the price below his costs.

The whole idea

A derivative is a tool for moving risk.

From someone who fears it — to someone willing to bear it.

Plant it cleanly and let it breathe. This is the callback target for the entire talk: every instrument we meet is a variation on this one sentence.

Movement 2 · The staircase

Forwards: Lock in a price with a handshake.

No matter what happens with the market, the price will stay the same.

He agrees a price today for grain delivered after harvest — the price you can lock in today for delivery later (never "what the market thinks prices will be"; that conflation is the most common derivatives misconception). Teach the axes here: across is time, from today to harvest; up is price. The locked price is the same number on every day of the season, so it draws as a flat, solid line. Whatever the market does between now and harvest, that line doesn't move. Certainty looks flat.

Movement 2 · The staircase

The same handshake, different seasons.

Whatever the world does, his number doesn't move.

Step through the seasons one at a time — each is one possible world, and only one is on screen at once. The dashed line is the spot price wandering from today to harvest; the flat line is the price he locked. Crash → the spot ends below the lock, so the forward saved him. Soar → the spot ends above the lock, so it cost him. Near → barely a difference, just peace of mind. The point isn't winning or losing — it's that his number stopped moving.

Movement 2 · The staircase

Futures: the same deal, between strangers.

With an intermediary, they need not trust each other — only the clearinghouse. Same chart. New plumbing.

But it's still a cage. Yes, guarding against downside, but a cage preventing exposure to a soaring market.

A future is a forward, standardized and traded on an exchange. The clearinghouse stands between two strangers and guarantees the trade, so they don't have to know or trust each other. Stress that the economics — the same temporal chart — are identical to the forward; only the plumbing changed. The remaining ache is the same cage, which the option finally opens.

Movement 2 · The staircase

Options: the right, not the obligation.

Below the strike, a floor protects him — just like the forward.

But once the spot climbs past it, his outcome lifts off and rides the market up.

All it ever cost him was the premium.

He pays a premium up front for the right — not the obligation — to sell at the strike. First beat: below the strike he has a floor, just like the forward, sitting a touch lower because of the premium he paid. Then the beat that matters: this season the spot climbs, and the moment it crosses the strike his outcome line lifts off the flat and rides the market up — the cage opens. The faded flat line is where a forward would have trapped him. The only price was the premium.

Movement 2 · The staircase

It wins in both seasons — minus the premium.

An option is insurance.

A premium for protection

Same two seasons as the forward, one at a time. In the crash, the spot sinks well below the strike and the floor holds — he's protected exactly like the forward, just a premium lower (the small gap below the strike). In the soar, his outcome lifts off and rides the market up; at harvest he gives back only the premium (the little gap between the spot and his line). So it wins both ways. Name the rhyme out loud: an option is insurance — a premium for protection — which sets up the CDS later.

Two flavors of option

Put The Buyer

Our farmer: protecting himself by locking in the right to sell at a predetermined price.

The right, never the obligation, to sell at a locked price.

The seller

Sells the farmer his insurance against price dropping — gets the premium, betting the price holds up.

Their payoff is the buyer's, flipped — the opposite shape.

Call The Buyer

An airline: dreading a spike in fuel prices before next season. They protect themselves by locking in the right to buy fuel at a predetermined price.

The right, never the obligation, to buy at a locked price.

The seller

Sells the airline its ceiling — and pockets the premium, betting the price stays down.

Same story, mirrored — the opposite shape.

Two flavours of option, same grammar. A PUT is the right to sell — our farmer's instrument from a moment ago. A CALL is the right to buy: meet an airline, terrified fuel prices spike before next season; a call lets it lock in a ceiling. Both are a right, never an obligation, bought for a premium. NOTE ON THE CHARTS: these are payoff diagrams, not the time charts from earlier — the horizontal axis is the market price at expiry, the vertical axis is profit. The put pays off when price falls below the strike; the call when it climbs above. On the right is the seller in each case: they take the premium up front and bet the option is never exercised — their payoff is just the buyer's chart flipped, the opposite shape. We don't go deeper than that here; selling options is a whole talk of its own.

From a moment to a stream

Some risk doesn't arrive just once.

The farmer's whole world was one harvest — a single moment.

But some risk returns — every month, every payment.

Meet the baker: a loan payment, due every single month.

Her risk isn't a price to bet on — it's a stream to manage.

This is the pivot. Recap the farmer: his whole world was one transaction, one harvest — a single moment, which is why a chart with one outcome could hold it. But some risk doesn't arrive once; it returns every month, every payment. The farmer can't show that, so we hand off to a new protagonist — the baker, who owes a loan payment every month. Notice the picture changed on its own: the charts are gone, and we're following a flow of arrows instead. We've left "betting on a price" for "trading a stream." Don't announce it — let it land.

Movement 3 · Streams of risk

Swaps: a bumpy stream for a smooth one.

The baker's loan floats — some months gentle, some months frightening.

They swap streams: she pays smooth and fixed; the bank takes the bumpy one.

The volatility didn't vanish — it moved to the party built to absorb it.

Two parties only for now. The baker has a variable-rate loan: her payment floats, jagged and unpredictable, and she craves a number she can plan around. The bank's revenue rises with rates, so a floating stream is its natural habitat. So they swap: she now pays a smooth, fixed stream to the bank, and the bank sends the floating one back. Watch where the jaggedness goes — it doesn't disappear, it moves to the bank, which is built to carry it. This is the key idea: a swap is risk reshaping, not risk shedding.

Movement 3 · Streams of risk

Who stands in the middle?

We drew the swap as a clean deal between two parties.

In reality a dealer sits in the middle — matching them, for a small sliver.

And behind the trade stands a clearinghouse — the intermediary from futures — guaranteeing it.

The two-party picture was a simplification. The baker rarely meets her perfect counterparty directly; a dealer — a bank's markets desk — sits in the middle, matching the two sides and keeping a small sliver (the spread) for the service. Watch the streams: each one now runs to the dealer and out the other side, two segments meeting in the middle. And behind the whole trade stands the clearinghouse — the same grown-up referee from the futures slide — the central counterparty that guarantees performance so nobody has to trust anybody personally. Dealer matches; clearinghouse guarantees. Only split them apart further if someone asks.

Movement 3 · Streams of risk

Credit Default Swaps (CDS): Insuring the thing everyone assumed.

A pension fund holds a company's bond — and dreads the company defaulting.

So it buys protection: a small, steady premium to a protection seller.

Then the unthinkable — the company defaults.

It's insurance — like the option. The one tool that insures what all the others assumed: that the other side pays.

The capstone. A pension fund lent to a company — it holds the company's bond, and its nightmare is a default that makes the bond worthless. So it buys a credit default swap: a small, steady premium paid to a protection seller — deliberately the same shape as the option's premium-for-protection. Then the company defaults; the bond collapses. And the seller pays one big contingent amount that makes the fund whole — many small payments, then one large payback. Name the rhyme: a CDS is insurance, just like the option. And it's the capstone because every instrument before it quietly assumed the counterparty would pay up — the CDS is the first one that insures that very assumption. It points risk management at trust itself.

Back to one idea

Five tools. One idea.

  • Forward — a farmer locks his price with a handshake.
  • Future — the same, but a clearinghouse lets strangers trade.
  • Option — he pays a premium to keep the upside.
  • Swap — a baker trades her bumpy payments for smooth ones.
  • Credit default swap — a pension fund insures against a default.

Every one moves risk — from someone who fears it to someone willing to bear it.

The roll-call. Step through the five quickly — don't dwell; each is just a variation on the same sentence. Then the payoff: the five recede and the refrain that opened the talk crystallizes. Five elegant tools, all one idea — moving risk from someone who fears it to someone willing to bear it.

One honest line

They move risk. They don't erase it.

Forget that — and things break.

Remember it, and they're simply a good tool: old, human, and commonplace.

The honest close. These tools move risk; they don't make it vanish — and when people forget that, things break. But don't end on fear, and don't give the 2008 lecture. End constructive: risk is just part of the world, that's life, and these instruments are old, human, and everywhere. The audience can now see them for what they are. Warm, unhurried — done.
